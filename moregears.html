Module 6: More Gear Types

Turns out, there are many gear types. 2 modules ago, we merely glossed over it. LEGO has its own renditions of it, and in this module, we'll take a look at each gear type along with downloadable Studio files demonstrating them.

Spur Gears

The spur gear is the most common type of gear. They are designed to transfer rotational motion with parallel axles (also called shafts). It is simple. Below is an example of a spur gear train.

Clarification

It might be tempting to say, "Well, why don't we just use double bevel gears all the time instead of spur gears and bevel gears?" The answer is not that complicated. If you've scruitinized a bevel gear before, you'll see it's not just used for perpendicular connections, it's designed for it only (in LEGO)! Bevel gears are also typically thinner than double bevel gears, meaning they can be used in space efficiency scenarios. This Studio file perfectly demonstrates how space efficient bevel gears are for their purpose compared to double bevel gears:

The same idea works with spur gears too. Spur gears are thinner than double bevel gears, and usually provide better parallel connections under the right circumstances.

Worm Gears

Worm gears are spectacular for achieving unprecedented torque without taking up much space and using too many parts. The follower gear when the worm gear is driving is usually a spur gear.

Knob Gears

These gears aren't commonly used, but they are similar to double bevel gears. Their most common use is for perpendicular connections. Their biggest advantage is low slippage, despite weak bracing/framing methods. It is partly due to the large teeth.

Takeaway

Using the knowledge you've learned from Professor Cheese, you can now choose which gear you want to use depending on the situation. It is very likely you might not want to use worm gears or knob gears at all. That's okay. Build diversity after mastery of a couple of gears.

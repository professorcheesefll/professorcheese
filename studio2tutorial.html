Module 2: Using Studio 2.0 for Planning

Studio is a CAD (Computer Aided Design) tool specifically made to help builders plan a certain creation, like a MOC, before ordering parts and building it in real life. For FLL, it helps students share their ideas without having to build something in real life first. Just imagine if you had to take apart your entire tool just to make a simple change! That would be strenuous, and doesn't yield results unless you are very experienced. In short, Studio is a designing tool optimized for efficiency.

  • If you are building with SPIKE Prime specifically (for FLL) you can switch between the "45678-1 SPIKE Prime Set" option and "Main".

If you cannot do the last bullet point, it means you do not have the SPIKE Prime set installed onto Studio. In order to access special parts, specifically from certain sets, you need to import them.

Colorizing Your Parts

While your team's performance may never benefit from choosing a "good" color for your parts, you should color-code your builds in Studio so others looking at it can easily identify and process it. Here are a few reasons why:

  • A team member trying to assemble your build can easily recognize what a certain part is and where to find it because SPIKE sets and LEGOs in general follow a color scheme for parts. This makes building easier.
  • A coach, mentor, or judge can familiarize themselves with a certain creation with their experience from real life.
  • Your build looks more organized, tidy, and neat, giving you self-esteem to build more and form connection with parts.

Connecting Parts Together

To connect parts together, you want to select the part. 3 axes should appear.

You now have 2 options. You may use the arrow keys on your keyboard to rotate the part in the right direction (to enable connection) or use the mouse to rotate the part manually. This is an example of the friction pin being rotated the right way in order to achieve connection:

Now, you can drag the part around until it is over the part you want to connect it with, and it should connect intuitively. You may have to drag you mouse down precisely to lead the part to the right spot.

Excellent! You should now be able to attach virtually any part to another, including axles, normal lego bricks, frames, and other exotic elements.

Use the Tools

Studio has specific tools that make the building process even more effective. Here is a small overview of each of them.

The "Select" Tool

By selecting the "Select" tool, you are able to select a part and drag it around. That is its function. Simple. It is also the most crucial one, which is why it is at the top of the list.

The "Hinge" Tool

The "Hinge" tool enables you to directly implement an element's x, y, and z axes with the level of rotation you want.

The "Move" Tool

Unlike the "Select" tool which also allows you to move a piece, the "Move" tool does it in a linear manner, while showing you the exact coordinates of a piece, improving control over the part of interest.

The "Clone" Tool

The "Clone" tool is like your copy-and-paste. It allows you to duplicate a part easily, to avoid using fancy keyboard shortcuts.

The "Hide" Tool

This tool allows you to hide chosen pieces. This can be useful if you want to think about whether you want to add a certain piece or not, or just to see a model removing the chosen part(s) without deleting them.

The "Connect" Tool

The "Connect" tool may seem counter-intuitive at first, but upon selecting it, you should be able to see cylinders structured around a part. This is a point of connection. Now, using the weird green line that follows your mouse, you can choose a second point of connection. This eliminates the need for strenuous dragging, which can be highly beneficial for long CAD design hours. Highly recommended!

Advisory Selections

Studio has some advisory selection options to guide you when you are building to give you a sense of how "correct" your CAD is.

The "Collision" Advisory

When selected, this prompts you to change parts that are physically impossible to connect the way you've connected them. Hence, "Collision" is the name. For instance, this is the current model without this advisory turned on:

The "Stability" Advisory

When turned on, this advisory shows you if your model falls down easily-- useful if you are building a robot, because that may signal a lack of weight further down rather than up.

The green area corresponds to how stable your build can be physically (a smaller area usually means it can fall down easily). Studio also shows clutch power issues and warnings. These represent any issues with the connectivity of the build, which is very important for performance at competitions.

Module 3: SPIKE Prime and LEGO Technic Tips

Thinking like an engineer and using Studio 2.0 are important, but you cannot do them efficiently without specific nuances in SPIKE Prime and LEGO Geometry. In this module, we'll learn about the SPIKE set and LEGO's Geometry. We'll also look at the fundamental errors made while using lego pins and axles.

Owning your SPIKE Set

The SPIKE Set contains the basic electronic components you'll need (sensors, wires, and the hub) plus some other non-essential LEGO Technic parts. On Studio, you can turn on "45678-1 SPIKE Prime Set" to see these essential components:

A Brief Introduction to Sensors

Humans can live without bumping into walls and stepping on tacks because we have sense organs. Without your eyes, nose, mouth, and ears, you won't be able to take inputs from the world around you. That's what sensors do for us. They take inputs, which our brain uses to take action accordingly. Robots, such as the ones you can build using SPIKE Prime have sensors, which take the input to let the hub decide what to do next.

The "Electric" Components

There are 4 electric components. One of them is the "Electric Rechargeable Battery" that gets attached to the Hub. The others are sensors such as the "Electric Sensor, Color", "Electric Sensor, Distance", and the "Electric Sensor, Force"

The Color Sensor

The Color Sensor's job is to detect color. Hence the name. You can use basic code to take in inputs from your color sensor if it is attached to your robot. This is what a color sensor typically looks like:

The Distance (Ultrasonic) Sensor:

The Distance Sensor's job is to detect the distance it is from a solid object. It uses sound that humans can't hear to detect distances effectively. Sound bounces off the solid object and returns to the sensor, and the time it takes for that to happen determines the distance.

The Force Sensor

While the Force Sensor isn't used very often in FLL, it has its own purpose. It can detect if something is touching the black knob it has at the front or not, and if so, how hard it is pressing on it. It uses the unit Newtons to measure how much force is being applied.

The "Electric, Motor" Parts

SPIKE Prime includes two motors. These components can turn items that are attached to them.

The Large Motor

The Large Motor is widely used on your robot for movement. This is because they are significantly more powerful than small motors for movement, and this allows them to live heavier things across larger distances more reliably.

The Small Motor

The Small Motor, on the other hand, is more often used for activating tools. They can turn gears on your tools easily and are typically built for that purpose. The SPIKE Prime Set comes with 2 of them.

Keep in mind however, that you may use the small motors for movement rather than the larger ones.

The Hub (Only Part in "Electric, Programmable")

The only element that is programmable is the Hub. This is where all of your code is uploaded, and it gets all of its ports connected to the motors and sensors you want to use. It also gets attached to the "Electric" part called "Electric Rechargeable Battery" to be operational, which is why it wasn't given its own image earlier.

The Hub is the most important part out of all your essential elements. Without it, no part of your robot can function. It is the brain.

LEGO Technic Tips

LEGO has interesting specs that you need to learn to make building more efficient.

The Units

This part below is 5 units wide. These units are commonly called "studs" or "modules". It is important to know what they are called because while referring to them, a name makes communication more effective.

Learning each part's geometry (module length) can lead to better spatial reasoning (visualizing a potential solution).

The Pins

Commonly, you'll find these two pins. They are different, and a lot of people mix them up. The one on the right is the non-friction pin. The one on the left is the friction pin.

For builds that don't need to be hinged and rotated frequently, the best piece to use is the friction pin. If you use a non-friction pin for the structure of a tool, then it might collapse or jitter frequently.

Here are more friction and non-friction pins. The ones on the right are non-friction pins, and the ones on the left are friction pins. You'll see a mix of 2 module and 3 module variants.

Axles and Pins are Color-Coded

While building, a pro-tip is not just to blindly keep measuring axles and pins. As you've seen in the image from before, the non-friction pins are all in shades of tan and light-bluish grey, while the friction ones are all in the black and blue shades. Axles have the same idea. The odd module lengths are all light-bluish grey or yellow typically, while the even module lengths are all black or red.

The odd length axles are on the right, while the even lengths are on the left. When you pick these parts up, knowing this subtle difference can help you identify them quicker.

Illegal Techniques

Illegal techniques will not get you arrested, but they'll leave your building at rest. They can make parts loosen, bend, deform, and even pop after lots of stress.

An illegal tehcnique occurs when a part is being stressed beyond its capabilities (in design). There are many examples of illegal techniques, and your job is to not use them at all because they can waste debugging time and valuable technician practicing later.

Detecting Illegal Techniques

Not everything can be detected visually, so here are some commonalities:

  • If a contraption feels hard to put together (parts resist to do be built together), it is highly likely that it's an illegal technique.
  • The contraption easily loosens over time.
  • The effectiveness of the contraption is mediocre compared to confirmed methods and lacks consistency.
  • The parts in the contraption crack and break over time.

Remember to keep an eye on these issues.
